Africa

I'm currently reading a book on modern African history, The Fate of Africa, by Martin Meredith. The book shows how postcolonial Africa was ruled by dictators and wracked by constant wars and mass killings. It's a primer on the 21st tragedy of the African continent and its peoples.

Now, I wonder: what lesson should we learn from this history? See, the failures of Africa are products of decades of colonial rule, with the natives being hastily handed the reins without much knowledge and expertise. Coupled with ethnic divisions and overly ambitious rulers who ascended to power through violence, and you have the perfect storm of seemingly endless cycles of tragedy.

Okay, so those are the arguments to derive from this grand narrative of African postcolonialism. But what lessons are there for the common people to learn? These tragedies are undoubtedly the result of people at the top vying for power and wealth. What could the ordinary people have done to stop the carnage?

Protests were time and time again suppressed, with violence. Elections, if they occurred, were rigged and marred with, again, violence. Coups were common throughout the continent. Numerous foreign interventions only exacerbated the already catastrophic situations.

There was, for the common folks, no way out of the misery. No matter how much they wanted change, ultimately the perpetrators of their suffering were too powerful to be dislodged. Each dictator reigned with impunity for decades, only to be violently dethroned by yet another tyrant who also reigned for long years.

I actually have no answer. To me it's more of an intellectual curiosity, these questions being fuel for much pondering. But it does not take away from the reality of African despair and tragedy. Maybe things will get better, hopefully. 21st century African history showed the worst of humanity. One can only hope that the light at the end of the tunnel is reachable.
